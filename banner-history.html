AL & WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS

FENWAY PARK · FOOTER BOARD
Fourteen pennants hang on Fenway's footer board: 1903, 1904, 1912, 1915, 1916, 1918, 1946, 1967, 1975, 1986, 2004, 2007, 2013, and 2018. Red pennants (WS) mark World Series championships; blue pennants (AL) mark American League pennants that did not end in a title. Tap any year on the homepage to jump straight to it below.
1903
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
91-47-3 · beat Pittsburgh Pirates, 5 games to 3

Boston's 1903 club finished 91-47-3 and won the American League pennant, then beat the Pittsburgh Pirates 5-3 in the first modern World Series, a best-of-nine series.

Key moments included Game 1, when Pittsburgh's Jimmy Sebring hit the first home run in World Series history, and Game 8, when Bill Dinneen shut out Pittsburgh to clinch the title. Cy Young, Bill Dinneen, Long Tom Hughes, and manager Jimmy Collins were central figures for Boston.

1904
AMERICAN LEAGUE PENNANT
No World Series played

The 1904 Red Sox finished first in the American League, but there was no World Series that year because the New York Giants refused to participate. So unlike most banner years, this one ended with a pennant but no championship series.

That makes 1904 unusual in Red Sox lore: it is a banner year on the wall, but not a World Series title season. It is still remembered as part of Boston's early dominance in the American League.

1912
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
105-47 · beat New York Giants, 4 games to 3 (1 tie)

Boston's 1912 team went 105-47 and won the American League pennant. They defeated the New York Giants in a World Series that ended in an eighth-game extra-inning classic after a tie.

The defining moment was Larry Doyle's error in the final inning of Game 8, which helped Boston win the championship. Smoky Joe Wood, Tris Speaker, Harry Hooper, and manager Jake Stahl were major contributors.

1915
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
101-50 · beat Philadelphia Phillies, 4 games to 1

The 1915 Red Sox finished 101-50 and won the American League pennant. They beat the Philadelphia Phillies in five games to take the World Series.

Boston's pitching, especially Babe Ruth's early-career mound work and the strength of the overall staff, helped carry the club. Tris Speaker, Harry Hooper, and Dutch Leonard were among the team's key names.

1916
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
91-63 · beat Brooklyn Robins, 4 games to 1

Boston went 91-63 and again won the American League pennant. They defeated the Brooklyn Robins in the World Series, four games to one.

Ruth's famous shutout streak in the postseason became a major storyline, and the Red Sox continued to lean on strong pitching and defense. Babe Ruth, Harry Hooper, Tris Speaker, and manager Bill Carrigan were central to the title team.

1918
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
75-51 (shortened season) · beat Chicago Cubs, 4 games to 2

The 1918 Red Sox finished 75-51 in a shortened season and still captured the pennant. They beat the Chicago Cubs in the World Series, four games to two.

This team is memorable because it was the last Red Sox championship before the long drought that became known as the Curse era. Babe Ruth, Harry Hooper, and Carl Mays were among the notable players, with Ruth also serving as a key pitcher before his legendary shift to offense.

1946
AMERICAN LEAGUE PENNANT
104-50 · lost World Series to St. Louis Cardinals in 7

Boston went 104-50 in 1946 and won the American League pennant. They lost the World Series to the St. Louis Cardinals in seven games.

The series is remembered for Enos Slaughter's "mad dash" from first to home in Game 7, a decisive moment that helped St. Louis win the title. Ted Williams, Bobby Doerr, Dom DiMaggio, and pitcher Dave Ferriss were major figures for Boston.

1967
AMERICAN LEAGUE PENNANT
92-70 · "Impossible Dream" · lost World Series to St. Louis Cardinals in 7

The 1967 Red Sox finished 92-70 and won the American League pennant in the famous "Impossible Dream" season. They lost the World Series to the St. Louis Cardinals in seven games.

Carl Yastrzemski's Triple Crown season defined the team, and the club surged from last place the year before to pennant winner. Yaz, Jim Lonborg, Tony Conigliaro, and Rico Petrocelli were key names.

1975
AMERICAN LEAGUE PENNANT
95-65 · lost World Series to Cincinnati Reds in 7

Boston went 95-65 and won the American League East and pennant. They lost a classic seven-game World Series to the Cincinnati Reds.

Game 6 remains one of baseball's most famous games because Carlton Fisk's walk-off home run helped force Game 7. Fisk, Fred Lynn, Jim Rice, and Luis Tiant were central to the Red Sox's unforgettable run.

1986
AMERICAN LEAGUE PENNANT
95-66 · lost World Series to New York Mets in 7

The 1986 Red Sox finished 95-66 and won the American League pennant. They lost the World Series to the New York Mets in seven games.

The defining memory is still Game 6, when the Red Sox were one strike away from the title before the game slipped away on Mookie Wilson's ground ball through Bill Buckner. Roger Clemens, Wade Boggs, Jim Rice, and Don Baylor were key contributors.

2004
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
98-64 · swept St. Louis Cardinals, 4 games to 0

Boston finished 98-64 and won the American League pennant. They swept the St. Louis Cardinals to win their first World Series title since 1918.

This team is famous for the ALCS comeback against the Yankees, when Boston became the first MLB team to win a best-of-seven series after trailing 0-3. David Ortiz, Manny Ramirez, Curt Schilling, Pedro Martínez, and Johnny Damon were major figures.

2007
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
96-66 · swept Colorado Rockies, 4 games to 0

The 2007 Red Sox went 96-66 and won the American League pennant. They swept the Colorado Rockies in the World Series.

Boston built on the 2004 core with a balanced roster and strong pitching, then dominated Colorado in four games. Dustin Pedroia, Manny Ramirez, Jonathan Papelbon, Josh Beckett, and David Ortiz were key names.

2013
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
97-65 · beat St. Louis Cardinals, 4 games to 2

The 2013 Red Sox finished 97-65 and won the American League East. They beat the St. Louis Cardinals in six games to win the World Series.

This team became a symbol of resilience after the Boston Marathon bombing, and the season was marked by strong clubhouse chemistry. David Ortiz, who won World Series MVP, was the emotional and on-field centerpiece, alongside Jon Lester, Koji Uehara, and Dustin Pedroia.

2018
WORLD SERIES CHAMPIONS
108-54 (franchise best) · beat Los Angeles Dodgers, 4 games to 1

Boston's 2018 team went 108-54, the best record in franchise history, and won the World Series. They defeated the Los Angeles Dodgers in five games.

The club was powered by elite overall depth, with Mookie Betts, J.D. Martinez, Chris Sale, and Xander Bogaerts among the standouts. Alex Cora's team was strong in every phase, from offense to rotation to bullpen, and it became one of the most complete Red Sox champions.

A note on 1904: it's the only banner on the wall that marks a pennant season rather than a World Series championship, which is why it stands out from the rest. The others on the wall represent either World Series titles or, in 1904's case, the pennant era of early Red Sox history.
