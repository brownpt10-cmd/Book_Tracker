RETIRED NUMBERS

FENWAY PARK · RIGHT-FIELD FACADE
The Red Sox display eleven retired numbers on Fenway Park's right-field facade: 1, 4, 6, 8, 9, 14, 26, 27, 34, 42, and 45. Ten belong to Red Sox greats; No. 42 is retired league-wide across all of Major League Baseball in honor of Jackie Robinson, which is why it appears on the wall even though he never played for Boston. Tap any number on the homepage to jump straight to that player below. For the full official record, see the National Baseball Hall of Fame and the Boston Red Sox Hall of Fame Register on Baseball-Reference.
1
Bobby Doerr
HALL OF FAME · 1986

Bobby Doerr was one of the great second basemen in Red Sox history and spent his entire major league career with Boston from 1937 to 1951. He made nine All-Star teams, won the 1944 American League MVP award, and was known as "the Silent Captain" because of his steady leadership and calm presence.

Doerr was an elite defender and a productive hitter, finishing with a .288 batting average, 2,042 hits, and 223 home runs. He missed the 1945 season while serving in the Army, returned to help Boston win the 1946 pennant, and was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1986.

4
Joe Cronin
HALL OF FAME · 1956

Joe Cronin was a star shortstop, manager, and later American League president, with a major league career that spanned 20 years. He won the AL MVP award in 1930, was a seven-time All-Star, and led the Washington Senators to the pennant in 1933 as both player and manager.

Cronin joined the Red Sox in 1935 in a major trade and later managed Boston to the 1945 pennant. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1956, and his number 4 is one of the permanent symbols on Fenway's right-field wall.

6
Johnny Pesky
NOT IN THE HALL OF FAME

Johnny Pesky was a contact hitter, slick infielder, and one of the most beloved figures in Red Sox history. He debuted with Boston in 1942, missed three seasons for World War II service, and returned to become a key part of the team's core with Ted Williams, Bobby Doerr, and Dom DiMaggio.

Pesky was especially known for getting on base and handling the bat well, leading the American League in hits three straight seasons early in his career. He is not in the Baseball Hall of Fame, but his legacy is deeply tied to Boston, including the naming of "Pesky's Pole" and the later retirement of his number 6.

8
Carl Yastrzemski
HALL OF FAME · 1989

Carl Yastrzemski spent his entire 23-year career with the Red Sox and became one of the franchise's defining players. He is best remembered for his 1967 Triple Crown season, when he led the league in batting average, home runs, and RBIs.

Yastrzemski was an 18-time All-Star and a left fielder known for his durability, defense, and consistency across generations. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1989, and his number 8 remains one of Fenway's signature retired numbers.

9
Ted Williams
HALL OF FAME · 1966

Ted Williams is widely regarded as one of the greatest hitters in baseball history and spent his entire career with the Red Sox. He won two Triple Crowns, led the American League in batting average six times, and finished with a .344 career average and 521 home runs.

Williams also served in the military during both World War II and the Korean War, which interrupted parts of his playing career. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1966, and his number 9 is among the most iconic on the right-field facade.

14
Jim Rice
HALL OF FAME · 2009

Jim Rice was one of the most feared middle-of-the-order hitters in Red Sox history and played his entire 16-year career in Boston. He won the 1978 American League MVP award and was known for his power and run production during the late 1970s and 1980s.

Rice finished with 382 home runs and a reputation as one of the premier hitters of his era. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2009, and his number 14 is now permanently displayed at Fenway.

26
Wade Boggs
HALL OF FAME · 2005

Wade Boggs was a premier contact hitter and on-base machine who played 18 major league seasons and won five batting titles. He broke into the big leagues with Boston in 1982 and spent the first part of his career building a Hall of Fame resume with the Red Sox.

Boggs was a disciplined hitter with elite plate coverage, and he consistently led the league in on-base percentage and batting average. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2005, and Boston retired his number 26 in 2016.

27
Carlton Fisk
HALL OF FAME · 2000

Carlton Fisk was one of baseball's most durable catchers and spent 24 seasons in the majors, including his early years with Boston. He won Rookie of the Year in 1972 and became forever linked with Fenway because of his legendary Game 6 home run in the 1975 World Series.

Fisk caught more games than anyone in history at the time of his retirement, and he was known for leadership, toughness, and handling pitchers. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2000, and his number 27 is one of the most famous in Red Sox history.

34
David Ortiz
HALL OF FAME · 2022

David Ortiz became one of the greatest clutch hitters and most important players in Red Sox history. Across 20 major league seasons, he hit 541 home runs and helped Boston win three World Series titles.

Ortiz was a 10-time All-Star and a postseason force, winning ALCS MVP honors in 2004 and World Series MVP in 2013. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2022, and number 34 now stands among the modern-era legends on the Fenway wall.

42
Jackie Robinson
HALL OF FAME · 1962

Jackie Robinson broke Major League Baseball's color barrier when he debuted with the Brooklyn Dodgers on April 15, 1947. He was the inaugural Rookie of the Year and later the 1949 NL MVP, helping transform the sport and American society.

Robinson finished with a .313 career batting average and was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1962. His number 42 is retired by every MLB team, which is why it appears on the Fenway wall even though he never played for Boston.

45
Pedro Martínez
HALL OF FAME · 2015

Pedro Martínez was one of the most dominant pitchers of his era, with elite fastball command and overpowering stuff. He won three Cy Young Awards and helped lead the Red Sox to the 2004 World Series championship.

Martínez finished his career with 219 wins, 100 losses, and more than 3,000 strikeouts. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2015, and his number 45 is the last of the retired numbers on Fenway's wall.
