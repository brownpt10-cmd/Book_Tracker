BOSTON RED SOX (51-48, 3rd AL East) vs. BALTIMORE ORIOLES (49-52) — DOUBLEHEADER

Fenway Park • Makeup doubleheader after Tuesday's rainout • Boston carries a 14-game winning streak in — Game 1 win ties the 1946 franchise record (15); a sweep would break it outright at 16
Game 1, 1:35 p.m. ET (11:35 a.m. MT): LHP Jake Bennett (BOS, 5-3, 2.35) vs. RHP Dean Kremer (BAL, 1-2, 4.15) — Kremer is 3-4, 5.04 ERA in 10 career starts vs. Boston; first career look at the Orioles for Bennett
Game 2, 7:10 p.m. ET (5:10 p.m. MT): LHP Ranger Suárez (BOS, 4-3, 3.15) — if activated — vs. RHP Kyle Bradish (BAL, 6-9, 3.61) — Boston had not named a Game 2 starter as of Tuesday night; interim manager Chad Tracy said Suárez is "in the mix" but Eduardo Rivera remains the fallback option

Next: Blue Jays at Fenway (7/24–26), then a 4-game set at the Athletics in West Sacramento (7/27–30) — trade deadline is Mon., Aug. 3
State of the Sox
  • Boston is 51-48 (3rd, AL East, 6.0 GB) with the winning streak frozen at 14 games after Tuesday's rainout — the record and the streak carry over unchanged into today's doubleheader.
  • At three games over .500, the Sox are as far above the break-even line as they've been all season.
  • Boston has won 19 of its last 21 and holds the American League's third and final Wild Card spot outright, 1.0 game clear of Seattle.
  • The climb continues: the Sox sat 15.5 games back on June 24, the low point of the season. Today, 28 days later, they are 6.0 back — 9.5 games of ground recovered.
Monday: Durbin Beats Baltimore From Behind
  • Trailing 1-0 in the first, 4-3 in the sixth and 5-4 in the seventh, Boston tied it on Ceddanne Rafaela's two-out RBI single, then Caleb Durbin broke it open with a two-out solo homer over the Green Monster in the eighth — his 10th of the year — off Tyler Wells (2-2).
  • Garrett Whitlock (5-1) worked a perfect eighth and Aroldis Chapman needed a leaping catch from Tsung-Che Cheng at short to close out his 22nd save.
  • Baltimore committed four errors and allowed two unearned runs; Payton Tolle allowed four runs with nine strikeouts over 5.2 innings in a no-decision.
  • Durbin is batting .351 (13-for-37) with two homers over his last 10 games, per CBS Sports/RotoWire. "You try not to think about it, but I want it," Durbin said of the record chase. "A lot of really good ballclubs have come through here, too… hopefully it's not done for a little while."
Rained Out, Not Rained On — Doubleheader Today
  • Tuesday night's game against Baltimore was postponed by rain (storms arrived roughly three hours ahead of first pitch; the postponement was called about 90 minutes later) and made up as a single-admission doubleheader today at Fenway — Game 1 at 1:35 p.m. ET, Game 2 at 7:10 p.m. ET.
  • A win in Game 1 ties the 1946 Red Sox — the Ted Williams/Bobby Doerr/Dom DiMaggio/Johnny Pesky club — for the longest winning streak in the 126-year history of the franchise. Sweeping both games would break it outright at 16.
  • Jake Bennett, already lined up for Wednesday's start, gets the Game 1 ball against Dean Kremer. Boston had not named a Game 2 starter as of Tuesday night; Ranger Suárez (groin) is "in the mix" per interim manager Chad Tracy, with rookie Eduardo Rivera the fallback if Suárez isn't ready. "We've just gotta wait and see how he feels tomorrow," Tracy said Tuesday.
  • Willson Contreras, on the streak: "It would mean a lot, for sure… we are relentless, we are resilient. If it happens it would be great for us and we'll have to celebrate that. If it doesn't, we have to keep going."
Shoulder to Shoulder With 1946
  • The 1946 Red Sox won the pennant on the strength of that 15-game streak (Apr. 25–May 10) but lost the World Series in seven games to the St. Louis Cardinals — a heartbreak finish sealed by Enos Slaughter's "Mad Dash" from first on Harry Walker's eighth-inning double in Game 7, with shortstop Johnny Pesky's relay throw home often cited as a beat late. Worth remembering on a day this club is chasing that same ghost.
  • Also from that summer: Cleveland player-manager Lou Boudreau debuted the radical defensive shift against Ted Williams on July 14, 1946 — the "Boudreau Shift" that still bears his name eight decades later.
Alone in Second Since 1946
  • Fourteen straight moved Boston past the 1948 club's 13-game run and into sole possession of the second-longest winning streak in franchise history. Only the 1946 team's 15-game streak, set that June, stands ahead of it.
  • Starting pitchers own a 2.63 ERA during the 14-game run; relievers are even better at 1.15, per Field Level Media.
The Two-Run Tourniquet
  • Hold the other team to two runs or fewer and this season is essentially decided: Boston is still 34-1 in those games (Monday's five-run Baltimore output doesn't qualify). The lone blemish remains the May 12 loss to Philadelphia.
  • Monday was the most runs the pitching staff has allowed in any game of the 14-game streak, but the staff still sports that 1.15 bullpen ERA and 2.63 rotation ERA across the run overall.
The 5-Run Recipe
  • Boston is 37-9 when it scores five or more and 14-39 when it doesn't — Monday's six runs kept the streak living on the right side of that split. Field Level Media has Boston averaging 5.9 runs a game over the 14-game run.
Fenway, Still a Riddle
  • Monday's win was a home game, nudging the home ledger to 22-27 against a 29-21 road mark — still a seven-game reverse split. Eight of the next 11 are at Fenway, so the window to correct it remains open.
Baltimore, Revisited
  • Monday's win pushed the season series to 4-3 Boston with two games left in this set, now both today. Christian Encarnacion-Strand, recalled by Baltimore from Triple-A four days earlier, homered and drove in a run for the Orioles.
  • The loss snapped the Orioles' season-best seven-game winning streak — making Monday's game a clash of the two hottest records in baseball entering the night.
  • Baltimore is thin behind the plate: rookie Samuel Basallo (sore right shoulder) sat out Monday and wasn't slated to start Tuesday, and starting catcher Adley Rutschman went on the 10-day IL Monday with wrist inflammation. "He's feeling a little bit better today. We're very hopeful with him," Orioles manager Craig Albernaz said of Basallo.
Odds & Ends
  • Blanked and blanking: Boston has thrown 10 shutouts and been shut out only four times.
  • Coin-flip baseball: still 3-3 in extra innings and two walk-off wins against three walk-off losses, but the one-run record improved to 10-12 with Monday's 6-5 win — the fourth of the 14 streak wins decided by a single run.
  • Cycle-logical: Monasterio's April 25 cycle at Baltimore remains the only one by a Red Sox hitter this season.
  • Month of July: 14-1, outscoring opponents 84-39.
Rotation Reinforcements
  • Suárez (groin) took part in Monday's throwing and pitchers' fielding drills but was held back from Tuesday's scratched start; his activation is still unresolved with the rotation that's been among the sport's best even though ace Garrett Crochet hasn't pitched since April 25.
  • Jake Bennett gets today's Game 1 ball against Dean Kremer; the next open rotation turn after the doubleheader is Friday's series opener against Toronto.
  • Connelly Early (left elbow) is throwing again with no return date set. Roman Anthony (finger tendon) remains in Fort Myers not yet swinging, and Trevor Story (hip/hernia surgery) is hitting and increasing defensive work with no rehab assignment scheduled.
  • Boston recalled reliever Zack Kelly from Triple-A Worcester Tuesday and immediately transferred him to the 60-day IL with right elbow inflammation — a procedural move that opens a spot on the 40-man roster ahead of the deadline; Kelly won't be eligible to return before early September.
Deadline Watch
  • Craig Breslow's stance hasn't hardened into a plan: "More time means we can get more information," he's told reporters — a sign the front office isn't rushing a call despite the streak.
  • Tuesday's Zack Kelly transaction cracked open a 40-man roster spot, giving the front office a bit more flexibility to add without a corresponding cut.
  • The only trade of the deadline window remains the 7/14 acquisition of OF/DH Jahmai Jones from Detroit (PTBNL).

RED SOX NOTES

THE FENWAY CHRONICLE
The 2026 Season So Far
From the cold open through June 24 — and the run nobody saw coming

Two months ago, the 2026 Red Sox were the worst team in the American League, and not many people in this town were arguing otherwise. The bats were cold, the losses piled up in that quiet, familiar way, and by the back half of June a proud franchise looked ready to pack it in and sell off pieces at the deadline. If you'd stopped watching, nobody would have blamed you. You could hear it in the voices of the broadcasters.

And then they won fourteen games in a row.

Not a hot week. 14 straight — a run that turned a lost summer into one of the most improbable stretches this club has put together in 80 years, and carried them to the doorstep of a franchise record set back in 1946.

So this is the story of a season with two completely different faces: the one that let us down for three months, and the one that's had Fenway on its feet ever since. Let's take it from the top — the good, the maddening, and the downright unbelievable — CAN YOU BELIEVE IT!

The Cold Open

To understand the run, you have to sit with how far down they'd gone first.

It began the way these things sometimes do — slowly, and then all at once. You'd settle in each night hoping this was the one where it clicked, and mostly it didn't. Young players and old hands alike who were supposed to anchor the lineup never quite found their footing, and just as you'd talk yourself into a little patience, the injury report would take the wind right back out of you. A talented roster on paper turned in one of the quieter, more disheartening opening acts this town has seen in a while. Hope of a new season, a new start, a chance, quickly fades into the grind of another losing season.

It settled to the bottom on one particular night. June 24. 32 and 46. Worst record in the American League, 15 1/2 back — and a baseball world already writing them off as deadline sellers.

That's where this story starts. It's not where it stays.

Cora Gets the Call to the Office

Before any of that, though, came the shockwave.

April 25. 27 games in, a 10-17 start, and the Red Sox fired Alex Cora — five coaches out the door with him. The first manager let go anywhere in baseball this year, and a man with a World Series ring at that.

Around the league they couldn't believe it. Rival evaluators asked out loud how you make a ballclub better by firing one of the best managers in it. And they've got a fair point, because everyone agrees on the thing that matters most about Cora.

The players loved him. He was a players' manager, true and through. That part is real.

The smarter version of the argument went like this: Cora can be a great manager and still not be the right manager for this particular team. The fire that lifted the 2018 club might be exactly the wrong match for a group this young and this fragile.

The national write-ups piled on the roster, too — a lineup near the bottom of nearly every category, a "run prevention" blueprint that had quietly become a polite name for not enough hitting, and plenty of voices insisting the whole thing was doomed before April was out.

Where I'll Plant My Flag

Here's my seat in the argument.

Yes, the players have to play — nobody's waving that away. And yes, he was a players' manager. But there were real problems with the way Cora ran a ballgame, and I watched every one of them.

The hook that never came, for one — pitchers left in an inning too long, sometimes two, as if he couldn't quite bring himself to make the hard call and wear it. Base-running aggressive to a fault, running the Sox clean out of innings. Errors in the field that chip away at a whole club's discipline, night after night. And this goes back to the previous season, so the players know this.

And the lineup card. That's the one that got me. It changed every single day.

Now, I understand the instinct behind it. He was hunting for a spark, for the one spot in the order that would finally light somebody up. But that constant shuffling has a cost of its own. Duran is one who needs to know how he fits. Story should have been dropped to the back third of the order sooner — and wouldn't you know it, he had some decent nights once he landed in the six-hole. Eventually they settled on Abreu and Contreras back-to-back, and for a minute there was a shape to it.

Crochet was already out. Roman Anthony was out. Then Story was out by mid-May. Later in June Kiner-Falefa goes down 19 June, and Mayer is out 26 June. And so by the 23rd, the 24th of June, if we're being honest, it felt like the air had gone out of the whole thing. Giving up the lead late on a Sunday in Denver was the gut punch.

The promise of this operation — strong pitching, strong defense — just wasn't showing up. Late-game losses. Errors on the grass. Cold bats. And a home record that was, and honestly still is, hard to look at. You kept waiting for the turn. Night after night, it didn't come. Little did we know at the time, Anthony Seigler who was called up from Worcester when IKF went on the IL, would become a piece of the spark or the fuel for the run.

The Spark Nobody Wants to Name

And under all of it, the injuries kept coming but they may have been the unintended catalyst.

Which brings me to the theory nobody's saying out loud.

What if the injury bug was the hidden spark? Young players thrown into the fire, carrying pressure they were never meant to carry — precisely because the veterans, Story among them, weren't holding up their end.

Sometimes a team has to be forced to find out what its kids can do. However it happened — design, desperation, or plain rotten luck — this one got forced into it. It felt as if Roman and Marcelo were thrust into the lineup as the solution. While we saw flashes from Roman, Marcelo's bat was a drag in the lineup. Combine that with lost potential in Duran and Durbin not settled yet, the lineup wasn't producing.

And Then, Fourteen Straight

Fourteen in a row.

That's the whole sentence. Let it sit there a second, because a few weeks ago it would have gotten you laughed out of the bleachers.

Out of the wreckage of that 32-46 ballclub — worst in the league on June 24 — the Boston Red Sox have won 14 straight, and 19 of their last 21. The coldest team in the American League is suddenly the hottest team in baseball. And Fenway has come back to life in a way you have to feel to believe. The dugout's alive. You feel it through the television.

It's Seigler who set the temperature — the attitude, the energy, the jolt this room had been missing since the first pitch of the year.

And Willson got fired up right alongside him. Literally.

You've seen a clubhouse catch fire before. Rarely do you see one do it with a fella practically vibrating out of his cleats in the dugout, dragging everybody else up to his frequency whether they liked it or not. They liked it.

Durbin's bat woke up on cue. Down to a red-hot Orioles club Monday night, two outs in the eighth, he turned one loose to left field — and that was the ballgame, 6-5, streak alive at 14.

Duran's still grinding. Not all the way back, but helping, and that's the point. And Masa keeps finding the timely hit, quietly turning into money in a part-time role.

The staff has shined all year, and now it's finally getting run support to work with. The bullpen quit handing games away the way it did in April. Small ball at the right time. Smarter at-bats, smarter reads on the bases.

And this is the part that gets you. This team does not give up. That's the brand of baseball we begged for in May, and it showed up in July.

Shoulder to Shoulder With 1946

Here's the number that raises the hair on your arm.

Those 14 wins put this ballclub in rare air with the 1946 Red Sox. One more, and they tie the longest winning streak in franchise history — 15. Even the 108-win team in 2018 topped out at 10.

It doesn't have to happen. This group has already given us more than we had any right to ask for a month ago.

And it'll have to wait a night, as it turns out. Tuesday's game got postponed — so the fifteenth win, and the ghost of '46, sit parked for now, pushed to a Wednesday doubleheader with the Orioles. Two cracks at history in one afternoon. You couldn't script the tension any better if you tried.

But wouldn't it be something to see.

What's Coming — and Who's Actually Deciding

Now, about that bat.

Here's a wrinkle you might've missed in the noise of the losing. After Cora's firing and before the streak, word got out that ownership itself had gotten into the act of hunting for a right-handed power hitter — not just Breslow doing the legwork, but the folks upstairs leaning in. Sam Kennedy, the man who has to stand at the podium and say the hard things, went on the radio and called these last months exactly what they were. Speculation started running on how long Breslow would last despite Kennedy publicly supporting him.

"Embarrassing, unacceptable, maddening, frustrating — whatever words you want to use. There's no way to sugarcoat it." That was Kennedy in June, with the club buried. A few weeks later he was warning that if things didn't turn, they'd have to pivot and sell. Then the streak happened, and the whole conversation flipped.

So the need is real and it's specific: a right-handed bat with thump, the kind Fenway's wall was built for, because outside of Contreras there just isn't enough of it in this lineup. And here's the honest part — Jahmai Jones isn't that answer. A red-hot cameo is a lovely thing, but three homers this year and eleven in a whole career don't add up to the middle-of-the-order force this club has needed since Opening Day. Fun story. Not the fix.

Which brings us to the question every die-hard is really asking. What has this front office actually done — and what will it do when the deadline comes on August 3rd?

You'll forgive a little skepticism here. This is a ballclub that has spent like a middle-of-the-pack team — right around twelfth in baseball in cash — while sitting on one of the sport's great money machines. The way plenty in this town see it, the Fenway engine prints whether the Sox win or lose: the network, the ballpark, the real estate, the whole empire. Forbes has the profit north of $120M a year. And John Henry, for his part, has made it plain over the years that he doesn't much care for selling assets or for what he's called the "false narrative" about his spending.

So I'll say what a lot of us are thinking, and you can hold me to it. I'm not convinced ownership is itching to open the checkbook, streak or no streak. The hope is that a winning team makes the math easy — that with the club suddenly a buyer instead of a seller, adding a real bat becomes the obvious, defensible move. We'll see if that's how it plays. Sometimes the will follows the winning. Sometimes it doesn't.

Jahmai Jones is a name in the conversation. He's not the one that matters. Watch what they do when it costs something.

PART II — THE TURNAROUND, GAME BY GAME
How a lost season got itself found — one night at a time
The Nadir in Colorado

Let's go back to the bottom. You have to, to understand the rest.

June 24, a mile up in the thin Colorado air. 32 and 46. The Red Sox carried a late lead into the back innings at Coors Field — and gave it away, three runs surrendered down the stretch, an 8-to-6 loss to the Rockies. Buried in the division. The whole baseball world already sharpening the word sellers for the deadline.

And then, because the baseball gods have a sense of humor, the night got worse.

The team plane broke down on the tarmac in Denver. Mechanical trouble, bound for New York, and instead of wheels-up they got hours of sitting — one delay stacking onto the next until they finally staggered into the city in the small hours of the morning. An exhausted ballclub, a lost night, and not a soul outside that room giving them a prayer.

Rock bottom. But out of bonding adventure, something happened.

The Bronx Statement

They came home to Fenway on June 25 and flipped the whole script over.

Four games against the New York Yankees. A four-game sweep.

It opened 6-3, the bats stirring early and Aroldis Chapman slamming the door out of the pen. Then Payton Tolle turned in a pitching clinic, 6-1, the Yankee sluggers waving all night. 4-1 the next day behind another strong start and one more Chapman save. And then the closer — a 10-inning walk-off, 5-4, Fenway shaking, the dugout suddenly believing something it hadn't believed in months.

Five wins in a row now, and a completely different team wearing the same uniforms.

Fireworks at Fenway — the Contreras Flashpoint

Then Washington came to town, and things got loud.

This is the series that lit the emotional fuse for the whole summer, and Willson Contreras was standing right in the middle of it — both feet, and a helmet.

June 29 he crushed a 3-run shot in the first and had the whole park roaring. An inning later, called out on a tight check-swing, he tapped the top of his helmet on the walk back — the signal for a challenge. Trouble is, that's not a call you're allowed to challenge, and gesturing for one is an automatic toss. Nic Lentz rung him up. Gone. The Sox won anyway, 6-3, number six in the row.

And then June 30 — the boil-over. They got walloped, 1-8 — the first crack in the good times.

In the 4th inning, Contreras strikes out looking against Cade Cavalli, and Cavalli barks at him on the way off the mound. That was the match to the powder. Contreras charged. Broke clean through the scrum, reared back, and threw his batting helmet straight into the pile as both benches and both bullpens emptied onto the grass. They would go on to lose the next game as well, 2-10. The sweep of the Yankees was now a distant memory.

Ejected for the second straight game he played — a first in the long history of this franchise — and a multi-game suspension to follow. 1-8 was the final that night. But something in this team had been welded together in that scrum, and it would show up the moment he came back.

The Streak: Out West, Then Home

After a breath on July 2, they went to Anaheim and lit the fuse for real. What followed is the part that's going in the record books: 14 straight, 37-48 turned into 51-48.

It was pitching that lit it. Sonny Gray was carving — 12-1 by the Fourth of July, pitching like a flat-out ace — with Bennett and Tolle right behind him, the rotation eating innings and sparing that young bullpen the nightly tightrope. A sweep of Angels in Anaheim. Then into Chicago: 8-1, a flawless 5-0 shutout from Bennett, a 2-1 duel.

Then to Queens, and the Mets — 6-2, a 4-0 gem from Morán, and a 3-2 thriller in the 10th to carry a 9-game tear into the All-Star break. Clean, fundamental baseball. Abreu banking the late insurance. The kind of ball we'd begged to see all spring, finally showing up every single night.

The Travel Nightmare, Take Two

And then the travel gremlins came back for an encore — because of course they did.

A runway tug malfunctioned Thursday night and trapped them on the tarmac past midnight. Friday morning it got worse: cockpit switches and lights gone haywire, seven more hours on the ground. They didn't get airborne until nearly three in the afternoon, touched down at LaGuardia at 4:30pm, and rolled into Citi Field with maybe two hours to spare before first pitch.

Show up and play. That's all. No excuses offered, none accepted.

So what did they do out of a doubleheader on no sleep? Bennett piloted a 10-0 demolition in the opener. And in the nightcap — here he came — Willson Contreras, back from his suspension, first inning, a home run to announce himself, a 5-3 win and the 11th straight. You could not make it up.

Grit, and a Blast from an Unlikely Bat

July 18, the streak into double digits, and this one took some sweat.

Down three early to the Rays, the Sox went to work the patient way, chipping at it. And then the spark came from a name you wouldn't have circled — Jahmai Jones, a game-tying blast that sent the dugout over the railing. The pen held the line, 7-6, comeback complete, series in hand. (Hold that name for later. It matters more than it looks.)

Gray finished the sweep the next day, 6-1. 13 in a row. On to the crown jewel.

Bedlam at Fenway

July 20. Baltimore in town, riding a 7-game streak of their own. Two hot teams, one packed house, and a ballgame that would not sit still.

Three separate times the Sox fell behind. Three separate times they clawed back, cashing in every Baltimore mistake along the way — Ceddanne Rafaela wheeling all the way around from first to score on a Wilyer Abreu double after an Orioles error, the kind of heads-up dash that tells you a team believes.

5-5. Bottom of the 8th. Two outs.

Caleb Durbin.

The same Caleb Durbin who'd been buried in a brutal spring slump, who'd completely reinvented his season — he got one and he did not miss it. Deep to left. Over the Monster.

Then a top of the 9th that stopped every heart in the building, and a defensive stand to seal it. 6-5. Bedlam — better than thirty-seven thousand of them on their feet, a ballpark that eleven weeks earlier had been chanting for the owner to sell, now roaring like it was October.

19 wins in 21 games since that miserable night in Colorado. 14 in a row. One shy of the 1946 club, and the whole city leaning in.

Last Word

This, right here, is why we do it. The cold Aprils. The abysmal home stands. The lineup cards that made no sense. You sit through all of it for a run like this one.

The angst was real. The disappointment was real. And so, now, is this.

So here's to one more, and to a ghost from 1946. Whatever comes next — win, lose, or heartbreak — we'll be right here in the next seat to experience every last pitch of it with you.

Let's go to the yard.
